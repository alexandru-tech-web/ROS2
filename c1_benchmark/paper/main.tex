\begin{abstract}
Middleware benchmarks for ROS~2 report performance almost exclusively under
ideal network conditions, yet search-and-rescue (SAR) deployments operate on
degraded links. We present a two-layer benchmark that measures
\texttt{rmw\_zenoh} and CycloneDDS under physically emulated degradation
(\texttt{tc netem}): a transport echo microbenchmark and a complete simulated
SAR mission with four UAVs. Across $2\times6\times5$ runs we find that the
two middlewares fail \emph{differently} rather than one dominating: under
30\,\% packet loss CycloneDDS converts loss into a uniform
${\sim}2.3$\,s latency wall and completes $5/5$ missions, while
\texttt{rmw\_zenoh} delivers the majority of samples faster but sheds
35\,\% of them and completes $1/5$; in the SAR-realistic combined condition
(200\,ms\,$+$\,15\,\% loss) their RTT distributions cross, with Zenoh ahead
on the median and behind on the tail. Transport tails degrade by up to three
orders of magnitude while mission time varies by at most 12\,\%, showing that
swarm autonomy compresses transport degradation up to an RMW-specific failure
threshold---an effect invisible to transport-only benchmarks. We release the
full benchmark, scenarios, and data for reproduction.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Communication is the weakest link of field robotics: disaster environments
combine long relays, interference, and non-line-of-sight propagation into
links with hundreds of milliseconds of latency and double-digit packet loss
\cite{murphy2014disaster}. ROS~2 delegates transport to interchangeable
middleware implementations (RMW)~\cite{macenski2022ros2}, and the recent
\texttt{rmw\_zenoh} offers an alternative to the DDS family designed for
constrained and wide-area networks~\cite{zenoh}. Existing comparisons,
however, measure throughput and latency on healthy networks
\cite{liang2023zenoh,maruyama2016ros2,kronauer2021latency}, leaving open the
question that matters operationally: \emph{what does middleware choice cost a
mission when the network degrades?}

We answer it with a two-layer benchmark. The transport layer measures
round-trip time (RTT) and end-to-end sample loss over an echo channel; the
mission layer runs a complete simulated SAR operation (four UAVs, ground
control station, area coverage, victim detection) over the same degraded
interface. Degradation is applied \emph{physically} with \texttt{tc netem}
\cite{hemminger2005netem}, so measured differences belong to the middleware
alone.

Contributions: (i) the first two-layer ROS~2 middleware benchmark under
controlled physical degradation, including the combined latency$+$loss
condition absent from prior work; (ii) evidence that CycloneDDS and
\texttt{rmw\_zenoh} embody two distinct reliability philosophies with
regime-dependent, qualitatively different failure modes; (iii) a quantified
transport-to-mission compression effect (tails degrade ${\sim}10^3\times$,
mission time $\leq$12\,\%) with RMW-specific failure thresholds; (iv) a
reproducible open infrastructure (scenarios, manifests, analysis).

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
Early ROS~2 evaluations characterized DDS performance and executor overheads
\cite{maruyama2016ros2,gutierrez2018realtime,kronauer2021latency}. Liang et
al.~\cite{liang2023zenoh} compared Zenoh, MQTT, Kafka, and DDS on throughput
and latency, finding Zenoh competitive---but exclusively on healthy networks.
Disaster-robotics literature documents degraded field links and their
operational cost \cite{murphy2014disaster}, yet does not connect them to
middleware selection. To our knowledge no prior work measures ROS~2 RMW
implementations under controlled loss/latency at \emph{mission} level; this
gap is the target of the present study.

\subsection{Transport microbenchmark}
A client publishes timestamped payloads (64\,B command class, 4\,KB
telemetry class, 64\,KB map/image class) echoed by a server on the same
host; $\mathrm{RTT}_i$ uses a single clock. Loss is the fraction of samples
not returned within a per-sample deadline. We report p50/p95 per (RMW,
condition) over $N{=}5$ repetitions of 20\,s per payload class.

\subsection{Mission layer}
Four UAVs execute a coverage-and-detection mission coordinated by a GCS
(Fig.~\ref{fig:gazebo}); telemetry, operator commands, and mission events all
traverse the degraded interface. Coverage is credited at the GCS only from
\emph{delivered} telemetry, making mission metrics causally sensitive to
transport quality. Metrics: time to completion (coverage $\geq$0.95 and all
5 victims confirmed; censored at 170\,s), final coverage, completion rate.

\subsection{Network conditions}
Six conditions span the SAR envelope: \texttt{ideal}; pure loss 5/15/30\,\%;
latency 200\,ms\,$\pm$50\,ms jitter; and the combined condition
200\,ms\,$+$\,15\,\% loss that approximates a relayed disaster link.
\texttt{netem} loss is i.i.d.\ Bernoulli; implications are discussed in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\textbf{H1}: under light degradation, RMW differences are operationally
negligible. \textbf{H2}: under heavy pure loss the two RMWs exhibit distinct
failure modes (uniform retransmission delay vs.\ sample shedding).
\textbf{H3}: the combined condition separates the RTT distributions
qualitatively (median/tail crossover). \textbf{H4}: transport degradation
compresses at mission level up to an RMW-specific failure threshold.

\paragraph{Three regimes (H1 supported)} Under 5\,\% loss or pure
latency$+$jitter the middlewares differ by tens of milliseconds and all
missions complete. Under 15\,\% loss Zenoh keeps p95 at 690\,ms vs.\
1056\,ms at a small reliability cost (2.3\,\% vs.\ 0.8\,\%).

\paragraph{Two failure modes (H2, H3 supported)} At 30\,\% loss CycloneDDS
trades delay for delivery---a uniform ${\sim}2.3$\,s wall, $5/5$ missions---
while Zenoh trades delivery for freshness: faster medians, 35\,\% samples
lost, $1/5$ missions, coverage stalling near $0.9$. In the combined
condition the CDFs cross (Fig.~\ref{fig:cdf}): Zenoh leads the median,
trails the tail (p95 4125 vs.\ 2523\,ms), loses fewer samples overall
(21.8\,\% vs.\ 34.1\,\%), and completes $4/5$ vs.\ $5/5$.

\paragraph{Compression (H4 supported)} Transport p95 degrades
$\times$1885 (DDS) and $\times$2547 (Zenoh) relative to ideal, while mission
time varies $\leq$12\,\% until the failure threshold. A spatial
log-distance channel with terrain profiles reproduces the effect: all 8
missions complete, T90 differs by $\leq$14\,\% between RMWs
($N{=}2$; differences within run-to-run variability).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
Middleware selection for SAR is regime- and stream-dependent: for
teleoperation and time-critical streams Zenoh's freshness wins until loss
becomes mission-threatening; for guaranteed mission progress under heavy
loss DDS's recovery wins at the cost of multi-second uniform delay.
\emph{Threats to validity}: \texttt{netem} i.i.d.\ loss is gentler on
retransmission-based recovery than bursty radio fading (conservative for the
Zenoh comparison); both endpoints share one host (no NIC/airtime effects);
the ${\sim}11$\,s Zenoh startup offset (present in \texttt{ideal}) is
attributable to router session establishment and excluded from per-sample
statistics; mission-layer spatial results use $N{=}2$.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
Physical-testbed replication over real Wi-Fi/telemetry radios (two hosts,
calibrated log-distance profiles), burst-loss models, adaptive QoS switching
on link state, and a hardware tele-impedance bench extending the methodology
to physical human--robot interaction.

\section{Conclusion}
A two-layer benchmark under physical degradation reveals what transport-only
studies cannot: CycloneDDS and \texttt{rmw\_zenoh} implement two reliability
philosophies whose failure modes differ qualitatively, and swarm autonomy
compresses transport degradation by three orders of magnitude up to
RMW-specific failure thresholds. Benchmark, scenarios, and data are released
for reproduction.
