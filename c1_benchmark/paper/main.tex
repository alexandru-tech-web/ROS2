\begin{abstract}
Existing performance studies of the Zenoh protocol and of ROS~2 middleware
(RMW) implementations report throughput and latency under ideal network
conditions. Search-and-rescue (SAR) deployments, however, operate over
degraded links with packet loss, latency spikes and jitter. We present a
reproducible benchmark that subjects \texttt{rmw\_zenoh} and Cyclone~DDS to
SAR-realistic impairments injected with \texttt{tc netem}, and measures their
impact on two layers: (i)~a transport microbenchmark (round-trip latency and
loss across payload sizes), and (ii)~a complete multi-UAV SAR mission
(completion time, coverage, operator-command success). [TODO: one-sentence
headline result.] All software artifacts are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
ROS~2 delegates communication to interchangeable middleware (RMW)
implementations. The recent Tier-1 adoption of \texttt{rmw\_zenoh} raises a
practical question for field robotics: how do Zenoh and DDS compare not on
ideal links, but under the degraded networks typical of SAR --- the exact
regime where middleware choice matters most? Prior comparisons
\cite{liang2023zenoh} benchmark Zenoh, MQTT, Kafka and DDS on unimpaired
100\,GbE links; mission-level consequences remain unquantified. We close
this gap with a two-layer benchmark under real, controlled impairment.

Contributions: (1)~an open testbed combining \texttt{tc netem} conditions
with an RMW-switchable transport microbenchmark and a full SAR mission;
(2)~measured transport curves for \texttt{rmw\_zenoh} vs.\ Cyclone~DDS under
loss (5--30\%) and latency (200\,ms, 50\,ms jitter); (3)~the first
mission-level quantification of RMW choice under degradation; (4)~public
artifacts for full reproducibility.

\section{Related Work}
\cite{liang2023zenoh} compares Zenoh, MQTT, Kafka and Cyclone~DDS under
ideal conditions, reporting Zenoh throughput up to 2$\times$ DDS.
\cite{mesh2024rmw} evaluates Fast~RTPS, Cyclone~DDS and Zenoh over a real
multi-robot mesh with dynamic topology, finding Zenoh advantageous in
delay, reachability and CPU usage; \cite{edgecloud2023} compares the same
families across network \emph{types} (Ethernet, Wi-Fi, 4G), where
Cyclone~DDS wins on Ethernet but Zenoh wins on Wi-Fi/4G and yields the
smallest trajectory drift on a real robot. \cite{automotive2025} extends
the comparison to automotive middleware outside ROS~2, and the ROS~2 TSC
evaluation \cite{tscrmw2021} qualitatively flags lossy-network discovery
as DDS's weak point. Crucially, all of these compare across network
\emph{types} or ideal links; none performs a \emph{parametric, controlled
degradation sweep} (calibrated loss/latency/jitter levels), and none
quantifies the consequences at \emph{SAR mission level}. Since prior
verdicts flip with network conditions, the open question is precisely
\emph{where} the flip occurs --- the gap addressed here.

\subsection{Layer T: transport microbenchmark}
Echo-based RTT (clock-safe across machines): a client publishes
timestamped messages (payloads 64\,B, 4\,KiB, 64\,KiB; 50\,Hz; 20\,s;
5 repetitions) and measures round-trip time on the echoed reply.
Metrics: RTT p50/p95/p99, measured loss.

\subsection{Layer M: mission benchmark}
A 4-UAV SAR mission (frontier exploration, cooperative mapping with
monotone acknowledgements, store-and-forward) runs unchanged under each
condition; the only impairment is physical (netem). Metrics: mission
completion time (95\% coverage, 5/5 victims), final coverage,
operator-command latency (sent$\to$ack$\to$done).
